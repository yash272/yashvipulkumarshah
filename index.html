Featured case · SwitchOn

Making AI inspection teachable.

DeepInspect needed a new dimension-detection capability to challenge competitors already strong in the category. The harder product problem was adoption: factory AI operators had to prepare images accurately before the model could classify good and defective parts.

Role
Product manager
Users
AI operators in factories
Outcome
Shipped to customers
Operator workflow · product decision
What happens next?

Before · open-ended

One image. One tool. Too much tacit knowledge.

Operators were told what to do, but the interface did not teach the sequence or explain mistakes. Training depended on an experienced person standing nearby.

Product risk More capability could create more confusion.

A dimension tool would add complexity unless the surrounding workflow changed too.

After · guided

A technical task became a workflow people could learn.

I changed the interface for dimension and anomaly-detection users, despite initial resistance to change, because a consistent guided flow reduced dependence on memory and made errors easier to find.

Selected decision Crop the part

Start with one bounded action, so a new operator always knows what to do first.

Signal

The feature gap was competitive. The adoption risk was human.

Customer conversations and internal teams showed that operators needed dimension detection, but the existing interaction model made advanced setup hard to learn.

Decision

Redesign the workflow, not only the tool.

I wrote the PRD and aligned designers, developers, AI engineers, sales, and marketing around a step-by-step flow with backtracking, guidance, and responsive feedback.

Observed outcome

Resistance gave way to easier onboarding.

The feature shipped. Customers reported a clearer flow and easier training for new operators, with observed faster completion and improved accuracy. No percentage was formally measured.

What I owned

From early signal to shipped workflow.

I carried the feature from an ambiguous competitive need through customer discovery, definition, cross-functional delivery, and the uncomfortable adoption questions that appeared after launch.

  1. 01 Discover Spoke with customers and sales and marketing teams to separate requested capability from the operator problem underneath it.
  2. 02 Define Translated the workflow, requirements, constraints, and adoption risks into the PRD shared with the product team.
  3. 03 Align Worked across design, software development, and AI engineering so interface decisions matched model and deployment realities.
  4. 04 Ship Led the feature to release, listened through initial resistance, and saw the guided flow become easier to teach and follow.

Founder postmortem · Bookchange

100 books. 2 exchanges. 30 people in a room.

I founded a peer-to-peer book marketplace and reached the final audition round for Shark Tank India. The sharpest lesson came from the gap between inventory and exchange: I built the marketplace before building the concentrated reader community that could make it move.

100books listed
2exchanges completed
30attendees at Books & Brews
What I believed

Enough supply would create the marketplace.

I spent limited runway building listings and exchange infrastructure while the user base remained too distributed to create dependable matches.

→
What I would do now

Build one reader community before building the marketplace.

Books & Brews drew 30 people after six months, when money was already running out. I would now begin with one concentrated community, test repeated exchange behavior manually, and let that signal earn the software.

Founder-level ownership includes the miss, the constraint, and the decision I would change.

Community first · marketplace second
